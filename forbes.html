    From Faith-Based to Evidence-Based: The Open Data 500 and Understanding How Open Data Helps the American Economy

    comments, called-out
    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments
    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments

    The mission of the Governance Lab, which I direct at NYU, is to improve people’s lives by changing how we govern, using advances in science and technology. At the GovLab, we’re studying the many ways that city, state, and national governments can benefit business and society by releasing the data government collects for free public use. Public funds have, after all, paid for their collection, and the law says that federal government data are not protected by copyright. By the end of 2009, the US and the UK had the only two open data one-stop websites where agencies could post and citizens could find open data. Now there are over 300 such portals for government data around the world with over 1 million available datasets. This kind of Open Data — including weather, safety and public health information as well as information about government spending — can serve the country by increasing government efficiency, shedding light on regulated industries, and driving innovation and job creation.

    It’s becoming clear that open data has the potential to improve people’s lives. With huge advances in data science, we can take this data and turn it into tools that help people choose a safer hospital, pick a better place to live, improve the performance of their farm or business by having better climate models, and know more about the companies with whom they are doing business. Done right, people can even contribute data back, giving everyone a better understanding, for example of nuclear contamination in post-Fukushima Japan or incidences of price gouging in America’s inner cities.

    The promise of open data is limitless. (see the GovLab index for stats on open data) But it’s important to back up our faith with real evidence of what works. Last September the GovLab began the Open Data 500 project, funded by the John S. and James L. Knight Foundation, to study the economic value of government Open Data extensively and rigorously.  A recent McKinsey study pegged the annual global value of Open Data (including free data from sources other than government), at $3 trillion a year or more. We’re digging in and talking to those companies that use Open Data as a key part of their business model. We want to understand whether and how open data is contributing to the creation of new jobs, the development of scientific and other innovations, and adding to the economy. We also want to know what government can do better to help industries that want high quality, reliable, up-to-date information that government can supply. Of those 1 million datasets, for example, 96% are not updated on a regular basis.

    The GovLab just published an initial working list of 500 American companies that we believe to be using open government data extensively.  We’ve also posted in-depth profiles of 50 of them — a sample of the kind of information that will be available when the first annual Open Data 500 study is published in early 2014. We are also starting a similar study for the UK and Europe.

    Even at this early stage, we are learning that Open Data is a valuable resource. As my colleague Joel Gurin, author of Open Data Now: the Secret to Hot Start-Ups, Smart Investing, Savvy Marketing and Fast Innovation, who directs the project, put it, “Open Data is a versatile and powerful economic driver in the U.S. for new and existing businesses around the country, in a variety of ways, and across many sectors. The diversity of these companies in the kinds of data they use, the way they use it, their locations, and their business models is one of the most striking things about our findings so far.” Companies are paradoxically building value-added businesses on top of public data that anyone can access for free.

    These include companies and entrepreneurs included in the study (and written about at greater length by Joel in Open Data Now) such as:

    • The Climate Corporation, which uses government weather and satellite data to shape new strategies for farmers – and which was just bought by Monsanto for about $1 billion.
    • Enigma.io, a startup that’s making federal datasets more usable and accessible, and OpenGov, which is making municipal budget data more useful to governments and the public.
    • Companies like Caclbench, Duedil,  and Analytix Insight that are giving investors more comprehensive, current, and in-depth information on companies by combining financial data from agencies like the SEC and data from other sources.
    • Consumer “choice engines” like the travel website Kayak, which uses flight information from the FAA to develop a better travel service. The company was recently bought by Priceline for $1.8 billion.
    • A number of healthcare companies that are building new businesses with both private and public health datasets.

    We need the business community’s help to develop a more definitive picture of the Open Data landscape for U.S. business. Participating is also a great way to share feedback to improve how government provides data that is otherwise often in obscure formats, technically difficult to work with, inaccurate, or incomplete.

    The GovLab is planning to use online and in person convenings and an array of annotation, commenting and feedback tools to bring federal agencies together with the companies that use their data, and work toward better solutions. I urge you to visit the Open Data 500 website, download the dataset of companies for yourself and share your experience with using public data with the growing Open Data community. Opening government is not inevitable. If we want governments to continue to share data freely and to invest in making datasets useful then let’s talk openly and specifically about how open data helps the American economy – let’s back up our faith with evidence of what works.
